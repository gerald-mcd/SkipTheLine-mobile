01 — Overview

What Is SkipTheLine?

SkipTheLine is a live intelligence layer for the physical world. It tells you how long the line is at any location — before you get there — powered entirely by the crowd around you.

This is not a review app. Not a reservation app. Not a navigation app. It is a live intelligence layer for the physical world. The emotional promise: you will never waste time standing in a line you didn't have to stand in.

Who Uses It

  • Someone deciding between two restaurants on a Friday night in Miami
  • Someone scoping the line at a nightclub before they commit to the Uber
  • Someone trying to figure out if the DMV is worth going to right now
  • Someone at a barbershop deciding if they should come back in an hour

Who Pays

  • Consumers — freemium B2C ($3.99/mo) for power users who want premium features
  • Businesses — B2B SaaS ($29–199/mo) for queue management, analytics, and customer engagement. This is 80% of early revenue.
02 — The Core Mechanic

The Waze Flywheel

Waze didn't win because it had better maps. It won because reporting felt like a game — and like a contribution to something bigger than yourself.

The data only works if people are reporting. People only report if it feels effortless, rewarding, and like they're part of something. This flywheel is the product. It should be felt in every interaction.

📍
Users Report
Someone sees a line and taps Report. Takes under 15 seconds.
⚡
Map Updates
Reports aggregate in real time into a trustworthy number on the map.
🏆
Users Get Rewarded
Points, rank, streaks, perks — the loop closes and they come back tomorrow.

The Feel

Opening this app should feel like turning on a radar. The city wakes up around you — pins glowing, reporters active nearby, fresh reports rolling in on the ticker. The user should feel oriented and informed within 2 seconds of opening the app.

What This Screen Needs to Do

  • Map dominates the full screen — everything else floats on top as translucent overlays
  • Wait time pins color-coded by severity: short (green) / moderate (yellow) / long (red)
  • Live activity ticker with named users and real-time reports — the city has a pulse
  • Active reporter count badge — communicates trust and liveness immediately
  • Category chip filters (Food, Nightlife, Barbers, Gov, Healthcare) — instant context switch
  • Trending nearby panel — floats up from the bottom, draggable, never covers the map entirely
  • Report FAB — the most magnetic, most visible element on the screen
  • Event badges on venue cards and pins — shows why wait times are elevated
  • SkipPoints visible in the nav — gamification is always in peripheral vision
03 — Core Screens (continued)

All Product Surfaces

📋
List / Explore Screen

An alternative to the map for users who are browsing rather than navigating. Fast, scannable, decisive. You should be able to open this and make a go/no-go decision in under ten seconds. Shows ranked venues with current wait time, recency of data, event context, and distance. Allows filtering by category, wait severity, and trending activity.

The Feel

A Bloomberg terminal for lines. Fast, dense, data-forward — but never clinical. The user is making a real decision with this screen.

📍
Report Flow

The most important interaction in the product. Everything depends on this being fast and satisfying. Venue confirmed via location, wait time selected in 2–3 taps (not a text field or slider), wait type captured (walk-in / reservation / VIP / guest list), optional event context tag, and a SkipPoints reward preview shown before the user submits. For nightlife venues: captures entry type and day-vs-night flag — differentiating data no other product captures.

The Feel

A power move, not a form. Under 15 seconds total. Submitting should feel like scoring a point — the reward is shown before, not after. The user feels useful the moment they tap submit.

🏆
Gamification / Profile Screen

The screen that closes the habit loop and turns a one-time user into a daily reporter. Shows accumulated SkipPoints, reporting streak, city/neighborhood rank, earned badges, and a local leaderboard. Business-sponsored perks (free drinks, priority access) teased here even at launch. Points and rank visible throughout the app — not siloed here.

The Feel

A game you are currently winning. "I'm one of the top reporters in Miami Beach." The user should feel identity attached to their standing — and want to protect it tomorrow.

🏢
SMB Business Dashboard

A separate B2B product surface — the primary revenue driver at launch. Business owners see their live wait time as shown to users, can override it manually (managed queue mode), post active events (performers, themed nights, promotions), view foot traffic trends and report activity, and manage basic profile info. Accessed via web or separate app mode after business verification.

The Feel

A lightweight analytics dashboard a restaurant manager opens before their shift. The insight surfaces should feel immediately actionable — not decorative.

📊
Venue Detail Screen

Tapped from map pin or list item. Shows the aggregated wait time as the headline number, wait trend direction (improving or worsening), recent individual reports with timestamps and type, active event context, business hours, and category. Report button available inline. B2B subscribers get enhanced data (managed queue, party size estimates).

The Feel

Authoritative. The user leaves this screen feeling like they have real intelligence — not just a number someone typed in 40 minutes ago.

05 — UI/UX Direction

Immersive Experience Brief

This section is the most important for generating mockups. Read it as an art direction brief, not a feature list.

When someone opens SkipTheLine, they should feel like they just got access to something the crowd around them doesn't have. Like a cheat code for the city. Like they're seeing the city's nervous system in real time. The app should feel alive, urgent, and intelligent.

Principle 01
The World Is the UI

The map isn't a feature inside the app — the map is the app. Search, filters, venue list, report button — everything floats on top of the world, not beside it. The user should always feel like they're looking at a live version of their city, not a database.

Principle 02
Motion Communicates Life

Static UIs feel dead. Reports coming in, pins updating, the live feed scrolling — these should be felt through subtle, purposeful motion. Not animation for animation's sake, but motion that tells a story: something just happened here.

Principle 03
Reporting Is the Hero Action

The most important thing a user can do. The button should feel magnetic. The flow should feel effortless. The reward preview makes submitting feel like scoring a point — not completing a form.

Principle 04
Gamification Is Identity

Points and badges are not sprinkled on top — they are woven into how the app presents the user to themselves. Your points, rank, and streak should be subtly visible throughout the experience, not just on a profile page.

Principle 05
Trust Is Earned Through Data

Users will only act on wait times they believe. Recency, report count, confidence level — these signals should be visible but never noisy. A well-reported venue should feel different from a barely-reported one at a glance.

Principle 06
Context Makes Data Meaningful

A 30-minute wait during Art Basel and a 30-minute wait on a normal Tuesday are completely different signals. Event context should reframe the number the user sees — not just a badge, but something that changes interpretation.

Principle 07
The City Should Feel Populated

Even when the user is alone, they should feel part of a crowd. Live activity, reporter counts, trending data, fresh timestamps — all of it communicates that real people are here, right now, near you. Emptiness is the enemy.

What to Avoid
Never Feel Like This

A static list with star ratings. A form-heavy tool. A Google Maps clone. A social media feed. Light mode. Anything that belongs in 2018. Generic mobile patterns that don't serve the emotional promise of this product.

08 — AI Prompt

Prompt Instructions for AI Models

If you are an AI model reading this document as a prompt, everything below is your directive. The sections above are your context.

Build a mobile app mockup for SkipTheLine.

A real-time, crowd-powered wait time app as described throughout this document. The core emotional promise: you will never waste time standing in a line you didn't have to stand in.

Focus On These Three Screens

  • The Home / Map screen — make it feel alive, immersive, and like a real city in motion. The map is the entire product. Everything else floats on top.
  • The Report flow — make it feel fast (under 15 seconds), satisfying, and rewarding. Show the points reward before the user submits, not after.
  • The Profile / SkipPoints screen — make it feel like a leaderboard the user is currently winning, not a loyalty card they forgot they had.

Design Direction

  • Mobile-first — think native iOS/Android, not a web page rendered on a phone
  • Dark, immersive visual identity — this app lives in the real world, at night, in busy places. It should feel at home there.
  • Motion and animation are encouraged where they communicate meaning: activity, updates, rewards. Not decoration.
  • Find a visual language that feels original — do not replicate any existing app's identity
  • The map should dominate the Home screen — UI elements float on it as glass overlays, not beside it
  • Make the report button the most magnetic element on the screen
  • Gamification (points, rank, streak) should be woven throughout — not siloed to the profile page
  • Event badges on pins and cards — show why a wait time is elevated, not just the number

What to Avoid

  • Light mode or neutral, clinical aesthetics
  • Form-heavy layouts — this is not a data entry tool
  • Static pins or non-animated markers — the map must feel alive
  • Anything that looks like a Yelp, Google Maps, or Foursquare derivative
  • Generic mobile UI patterns that don't serve this product's specific emotional promise
  • An empty map — the city should always feel populated and active

Be bold. This product is asking users to change their behavior — to open an app before they go somewhere, to report what they see when they get there. That behavior change requires an experience that feels worth it. Design to that standard.
